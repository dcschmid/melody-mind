## About the Album

Onder de Scheldelucht is a harbor record before it is a history record. Its world is made
of wet stone, night shifts, crane light, cassette hiss, and the flat silver pull of the
Scheldt. The music sits between Flemish indie rock, atmospheric folk rock, and grey
alt-pop: rain-soaked guitars, warm bass, muted drums, occasional accordion color, and
synth haze that hangs low over the water.

"De Rivier Herinnert" gives the album its voice. The river remembers, but not in a
sentimental way. It carries work, trade, family, departure, and whatever people throw into
silence because they do not know where else to put it. "Nacht aan de Kaaien" and "Kransen
van Kraanlicht" move through the modern harbor as a place of beauty and fatigue, where
artificial light can look almost holy after midnight.

The father’s tapes give the record its human center. "Cassettes van Mijn Vader" turns old
magnetic noise into inheritance: not wisdom exactly, but a voice with gaps in it. "Goud in
de Modder" catches the album’s best contradiction. There is money, work, and pride in the
mud, but there is also grime that never fully washes off. "Na de Val" keeps the tone low,
less interested in spectacle than in what remains after a city or a family loses its old
certainty.

"Treinen Naar het Noorden" expands the map without leaving the river behind. Movement is
everywhere on this album: freight, workers, rumors, lovers, ships, old names. "Werfgebed"
is one of the quieter pieces, a dockside prayer without much ceremony. "Regenstad Liefde"
gives the record a love song, though even that love seems to happen under bad weather and
fluorescent light.

The later tracks pull the harbor into a wider, lonelier system. "Global Line Blues" is
about the anonymous flow of goods and the people who keep it moving. "Café Zonder Namen"
understands the comfort of rooms where nobody asks too many questions. "De
Smokkelaarsmaan" lets the album flirt with shadow economy and folklore, but it stays
grounded in the river’s practical darkness.

"Laatste Shift" is the emotional release point. It sounds like a goodbye not only to a
job, but to a way of being useful. "Eén Licht op de Schelde" closes the album with a small
image instead of a grand answer. One light on the water is not redemption. It is enough to
keep looking.

Onder de Scheldelucht works because it does not polish the harbor into romance. It lets
the river stay grey, useful, dirty, and strangely tender. The album’s best moments
understand that memory in a working city rarely arrives cleanly. It comes through tape
hiss, wet shoes, unnamed cafés, and the glow of cranes after the last shift has ended.
