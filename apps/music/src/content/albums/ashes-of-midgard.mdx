## About This Album

Ashes of Midgard is an epic and atmospheric Viking Metal album that tells the story of a
dying northern world consumed by fire, frost, ruin, and memory. Across 12 powerful tracks,
the album unfolds like a final saga carved into stone and ash, blending crushing riffs,
thunderous drums, mournful melodies, deep commanding vocals, fierce growls, and dark
cinematic grandeur into one cohesive journey through the fall of a shattered realm.

The album opens with “Crown of Embers,” a towering introduction to the world of Ashes of
Midgard. It presents the image of a burned king standing before the ruins of his homeland,
still carrying pride and dignity even as everything around him collapses. That tragic
sense of nobility sets the emotional tone for the record. From there, “Wolves Beneath the
Storm” pushes forward with more force and aggression, following warriors marching through
snow and darkness toward a battle they know may be their last. It captures the fierce,
relentless side of the album — the sound of courage sharpened by doom.

With “When the Longships Burn,” the record sinks deeper into loss and memory. The
destruction is no longer only happening in battle; it becomes personal, historical, and
almost spiritual. The image of longships burning on a black shore turns the fall of
Midgard into something larger than war — it becomes the end of identity itself. That
feeling continues in “Ravens Over Broken Stone,” where shattered halls, broken monuments,
and circling ravens paint a cold vision of aftermath. It is a song of ruin and silence,
where kingdoms survive only as echoes in snow and stone.

At the heart of the album lies “The Last Mead Hall,” one of its most emotional moments.
Here, the focus shifts from battle to farewell. Firelight, memory, brotherhood, and the
presence of the dead all gather in a final meeting before the darkness closes in. It is
not a song of victory, but of remembrance. That fragile emotional warmth is then crushed
by the devastating weight of “No Gods in the Ashes,” one of the bleakest tracks on the
album. In this chapter, faith itself breaks apart. Survivors walk through a destroyed
world and realize that the gods have gone silent. What remains is not hope, but defiance.

“Frost on the Fallen” deepens the album’s sense of grief. It is a lament for warriors lost
in the final clash, covered not in glory but in frost and silence. The song gives the
album one of its most beautiful and mournful moments, emphasizing loyalty, sacrifice, and
the burden carried by those left behind. In contrast, “Banner of the Dying North” rises
with a more martial and anthemic spirit. It is the sound of one final banner raised over a
collapsing kingdom — not as a symbol of triumph, but as an act of resistance. Even at the
edge of extinction, pride still stands.

The world of Ashes of Midgard becomes even more desolate with “Shores of Black Snow,” a
haunting and atmospheric track that expands the album’s sense of place. Its blackened
coastlines, cold wind, ash-covered landscapes, and lingering silence give the record a
mythic, almost dreamlike sense of devastation. Then “Iron Oath at Twilight” restores
movement and urgency, capturing the moment before the final assault. Here, warriors swear
themselves to fate beneath a dying sky, and the album returns to themes of resolve,
sacrifice, and honor in the face of certain loss.

As the album approaches its end, “Beyond the Wolf Moon” opens the story into something
more mystical and transcendent. The world of ruin gives way to something more symbolic — a
crossing beyond battle, beyond death, and beyond the mortal realm. It is one of the
album’s most mythological songs, where grief begins to transform into legend. Finally,
“Fire in Valhalla’s Wind” brings everything to its climactic conclusion. This closing
track gathers the album’s central themes — war, loss, memory, pride, fire, and immortality
— and lifts them into one final, towering ending. It feels less like a simple finale and
more like the last breath of a world becoming myth.

What makes Ashes of Midgard powerful is the way it balances heaviness with atmosphere, and
destruction with emotion. This is not just an album about battle. It is about what remains
after battle: broken halls, dead companions, abandoned gods, scorched banners, frozen
graves, and the stubborn refusal to vanish without a voice. Every song adds another piece
to that picture, making the album feel like a complete narrative rather than a collection
of isolated tracks.

Musically, the record embraces the raw force of Viking Metal while expanding it with
cinematic scale and immersive storytelling. Massive choruses rise like funeral hymns.
Guitars shift between crushing weight and cold melancholy. Drums strike like distant
thunder across mountains and ruined coastlines. Choirs and atmospheric layers give the
music an ancient, mythic presence, turning each track into a chapter of a larger northern
elegy. The result is an album that feels both intimate and enormous — grounded in grief,
but written on the scale of legend.

Ashes of Midgard is for listeners who want Viking Metal that is not only heavy, but
meaningful; not only epic, but emotionally resonant. It is the sound of a world burning
beneath storm-black skies, and of voices rising through the ashes so they will not be
forgotten.
