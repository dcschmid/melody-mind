## About the Album

Everything Is Priority One turns the familiar language of product management into a
full-scale corporate power-metal drama. Roadmaps become battle plans, stakeholder meetings
resemble war councils, and a harmless-looking customer ticket develops the destructive
potential of an architectural siege weapon. Beneath the comedy, however, lies a remarkably
precise story about responsibility, indecision and the damage caused when leadership
mistakes universal agreement for genuine strategy.

The album follows Leon Brandt, newly promoted to Head of Product for Project Phoenix. His
assignment sounds difficult but manageable: define a clear direction, coordinate the teams
and deliver a platform customers can trust. For a few brief hours, Leon even succeeds.
Opener “The Product Vision” introduces him in front of three glowing monitors, laser
pointer raised, presenting a disciplined roadmap built around measurable goals. The heroic
guitars and confident refrain suggest that order has finally arrived. Then management,
Sales, Marketing and customers begin contributing ideas.

From that point onward, the album escalates with the ruthless efficiency of a calendar
filling itself while nobody is looking. The title track, “Everything Is Priority One,” is
the record’s central anthem: an enormous, immediately memorable chorus built around the
absurd corporate belief that every task can be treated as the most important task. Sales
has a critical deal. Marketing needs a relaunch. Management has already announced an AI
feature. QA needs time to stabilize the existing product. Every request is reasonable when
heard alone, and completely impossible when accepted together.

That tension gives the album its comic force. The jokes are not random office references
pasted onto conventional metal songs. They emerge from recognizable behavior: promises
made before estimates exist, hypothetical customers reshaping real development work and
roadmaps repeatedly renamed “Final” without ever becoming final. “Just One Small Feature”
begins with a simple field request and gradually exposes the permissions, migrations,
audit requirements and testing obligations hidden beneath it. Its progressive rhythmic
changes reflect the expanding technical consequences, allowing the arrangement itself to
become part of the joke.

“The Roadmap Is a Living Document” takes a more reflective approach by giving the roadmap
its own voice. What begins as a rational plan becomes an archive of revisions, duplicate
commitments and abandoned intentions. The song’s wit carries a quiet sadness: the document
remembers having a purpose, even when the people editing it no longer do. That mixture of
humor and consequence is one of the album’s greatest strengths. It laughs at corporate
dysfunction without pretending the pressure on the people inside it is imaginary.

Musically, Everything Is Priority One is rooted in comedy power metal, but its identity is
strengthened by progressive-rock structures and hard-rock confrontation. Twin guitars
provide the heroic charge, while Hammond organ, melodic bass and detailed acoustic drums
keep the arrangements mobile and expressive. Time-signature changes are used to illustrate
shifting requirements rather than to advertise technical cleverness. Arena-sized choruses
give the slogans room to land, while the more complex sections show what happens after
those slogans collide with reality.

A recurring four-note motif runs through the album. At first it sounds like a triumphant
corporate fanfare, perfectly suited to a polished product presentation. As Leon loses
control, the motif becomes fragmented, extended and increasingly unstable. When the team
finally agrees on a workable direction, those four notes return in a simpler and more
resolved form. It is a small compositional device, but it helps the record feel like one
deliberately constructed album rather than fourteen unrelated workplace songs.

The middle of the story delivers the record’s most extravagant satire. “Strategic Priority
One” finds Leon inventing additional priority levels because the existing system can no
longer distinguish between competing emergencies. The titles become increasingly elaborate
while the actual decisions remain unmade. “The Backlog Never Forgets” then slows the pace
and transforms 2,400 stored tickets into a collective memory of every postponed
confrontation. The backlog is not portrayed as the villain. It merely preserves what the
company refused to decide.

The first major collapse arrives with “Feature Creep.” Project Phoenix now contains
portals, predictive tools, custom tax logic, private-cloud options and enough
configuration to bury its original purpose. The track drives forward with the album’s
heaviest momentum, while the lyrics trace dependencies with unnerving specificity. Each
addition sounds manageable; together they make the product impossible to release. This is
where the humor begins to tighten into genuine tension.

Leon’s darkest failure comes in “The Customer Who Might Sign.” A major prospect has not
approved a price, completed procurement or defined its requirements, yet the entire
roadmap begins bending around the possibility of a contract. The probability of success
rises even as the evidence becomes weaker. It is a sharp portrait of organizations
gambling stable products on imagined revenue.

The album’s emotional turning point belongs to Sabine in “No Is Also a Product Decision.”
Rather than offering Leon another framework, she identifies the real issue: he has used
agreement to avoid accountability. Every yes preserved a meeting but transferred the
consequences to the team. The duet strips away the heroic language and forces Leon to
recognize that strategy requires exclusion. Choosing one direction means allowing another
possibility to disappear.

That realization erupts in “Delete the Roadmap.” During a major presentation, Leon removes
almost three quarters of the planned work. Sales objects, Marketing panics and Victor
accuses him of reducing ambition. Leon’s response defines the album: he is not shrinking
the vision; he is restoring a product. The song delivers the record’s largest
confrontation without pretending the decision is painless.

The final chapters are satisfying because they do not promise a magical cultural
transformation. “The Minimum Viable Truth” establishes a smaller, honest release. “One
Product, One Direction” lets development, QA, Product and Sales finally work from the same
reality. The closing “A Tiny Change Before Launch” brings back the familiar customer
request, but this time Leon refuses to compromise the release before the change has been
understood and tested.

Although the album speaks through power-metal hooks and progressive arrangements, it
shares those genres’ suspicion of empty authority, inflated promises and systems that
protect themselves at the expense of the people doing the work.

Everything Is Priority One is funny because its details feel true, but it remains
compelling because its central conflict reaches far beyond software. It is an album about
the courage to choose, the cost of pleasing everyone and the uncomfortable moment when a
leader realizes that indecision is still a decision. Come for the impossible roadmap and
the 2,400-entry backlog; stay for the hooks, the characters and a finale that earns its
hard-won sense of direction.
