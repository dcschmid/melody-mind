## About This Album

Minuit sur la Seine is an elegant and melancholic French chanson-pop album that unfolds
like a midnight walk through Paris. Inspired by rain-soaked streets, glowing reflections
on the Seine, empty bridges, quiet cafés, and the memory of a lost love, the album blends
classic French chanson atmosphere with a soft modern pop sensibility. With intimate French
vocals, delicate piano, subtle accordion, warm bass, brushed percussion, muted trumpet,
and cinematic strings, every track feels like a scene from a poetic nocturnal film.

This is an album about longing, silence, memory, and the strange beauty of walking alone
through a city that still feels full of someone who is no longer there. Paris becomes more
than a backdrop — it becomes the emotional landscape of the album. The river, the rain,
the lanterns, the cafés, and the sleeping windows all carry traces of love, regret, and
quiet reflection.

The journey begins with “Sous les Réverbères,” a graceful opening track that places the
listener beneath the soft glow of Parisian street lamps. It captures the feeling of
walking alone at midnight, where wet pavement reflects golden light and every step seems
to awaken old memories. The song sets the album’s mood: elegant, nostalgic, romantic, and
quietly heartbroken.

“Pluie sur le Pont Neuf” deepens the rainy-night atmosphere, bringing the listener onto
one of Paris’s most iconic bridges. The song evokes the image of rain falling on stone,
the Seine moving quietly below, and a lonely figure pausing between past and present. It
is cinematic, refined, and bittersweet — a perfect portrait of love remembered through
water and light.

With “Les Ombres de Montmartre,” the album moves through the narrow streets and stairways
of Montmartre after midnight. Closing cafés, distant laughter, and fading shadows create a
dreamlike scene where the past feels close enough to touch. The song brings a slightly
bohemian and nostalgic character to the album, adding another layer of Parisian romance
and melancholy.

The title track, “Minuit sur la Seine,” stands at the emotional center of the album. It
captures the stillness of the river at midnight, the black water reflecting the lights of
the city, and the quiet ache of a love that has ended but never fully disappeared. With
its refined chanson-pop sound and cinematic emotional weight, the song feels like the
heart of the entire story — intimate, romantic, and deeply atmospheric.

“Le Café Ferme Trop Tôt” turns the focus inward, telling the story of a late-night café
closing before everything could be said. Empty tables, unfinished conversations, and the
silence after goodbye create a delicate scene of regret. The song feels personal and
intimate, as if the listener is sitting at the last table by the window, watching the
night swallow the words that remained unspoken.

On “Asphalte Mouillé,” the album steps back into the city streets. Rain-soaked asphalt,
dim neon, and blurred reflections create a more modern nocturnal mood. This track adds a
stylish urban edge to the album while preserving its emotional softness. It is a song
about walking through the city while thoughts of heartbreak echo louder than the traffic.

“Une Lettre Jamais Envoyée” is one of the album’s most intimate moments. It imagines an
unsent love letter written late at night in a small Paris apartment while rain taps
against the window. The song explores everything that remains locked inside the heart:
apologies, confessions, memories, and words too fragile to send. Tender, sorrowful, and
poetic, it gives the album a deeply personal emotional core.

“Dernier Métro pour Personne” introduces a lonely urban rhythm, inspired by missing the
last metro and continuing through Paris on foot. The closed stations and empty streets
become symbols of isolation, but also of movement. The song carries a gentle pulse, like
footsteps through the night, and brings a quiet cinematic tension to the album.

With “Les Fenêtres Éteintes,” the city begins to fall asleep. Apartment windows go dark
one by one, and the silence of Paris becomes almost sacred. This track is restrained,
delicate, and deeply melancholic, reflecting on absence and solitude. It is one of the
album’s quietest songs, but also one of its most emotionally powerful.

“Rue de la Lune” brings a softer, dreamlike glow to the album. Set on a moonlit Paris
street after the rain, the song turns the city into a map of memories. Every corner
recalls a moment, every shadow carries an echo of the past. Romantic, nocturnal, and
luminous, it adds a graceful sense of wonder to the album’s melancholy.

“Ton Nom dans la Bruine” returns to the mist and drizzle of the Paris night. The song
imagines hearing a former lover’s name in the wind, in the rain, and in the reflections on
the street. With its haunting atmosphere and emotional vocal delivery, it becomes one of
the most bittersweet chapters of the album — a song about how memory can appear everywhere
when the heart is not ready to let go.

The album closes with “Au Bout de la Nuit,” a gentle and quietly hopeful finale. After a
night of rain, memory, loneliness, and longing, the first signs of dawn begin to appear.
The song does not erase the sadness of the album, but it transforms it. It suggests that
letting go does not mean forgetting — it means learning to carry love differently. As the
final track, it brings Minuit sur la Seine to a luminous, bittersweet conclusion.

Across its twelve songs, Minuit sur la Seine creates a complete emotional journey through
Paris after dark. It is a record for rainy evenings, late-night walks, romantic nostalgia,
and moments of quiet reflection. Elegant, cinematic, and deeply human, the album captures
the beauty of a city at midnight and the lingering ache of a love that still glows
somewhere in the distance.

Minuit sur la Seine is the sound of Paris when the crowds have disappeared, the bridges
are empty, the rain has softened the city, and the heart is finally honest with itself.
