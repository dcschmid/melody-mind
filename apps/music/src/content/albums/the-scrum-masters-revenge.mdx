## About the Album

Sabine Wolf joins Infinite Loop Solutions with a sincere belief in agile work. After a
serious security incident, the company hires a consultancy to complete an
organization-wide transformation in twelve weeks. Sabine expects the new process to bring
development, QA, and operations into the same conversation. Instead, management turns
every useful practice into another instrument for measuring the team.

The Scrum Master’s Revenge follows that change across fourteen linked songs. Progressive
power metal suits the early optimism: Hammond organ, layered guitar harmonies, and large
choruses give the transformation the air of an official campaign. Unease enters through a
five-beat timer figure that first sounds like ordinary meeting punctuation. As the process
hardens, the motif returns more often and leaves less room around it.

“Welcome to the Transformation” introduces Sabine before the language has gone sour. She
wants shorter feedback loops, visible problems, and meetings where quieter colleagues can
speak. “Fifteen Minutes Only” immediately tests those intentions. A concise stand-up grows
into a two-hour recital of status updates, side arguments, and managerial questions.
Clipped rhythms keep resetting the song before anyone can finish a thought.

“Story Points of No Return” finds the company assigning numbers to uncertainty, risk, and
individual performance. The satire becomes sharper on “The Backlog Never Dies,” where
finishing one ticket creates five more and urgent work disappears beneath presentation and
approval. These songs do not blame the board or the method by itself. They show how a
planning tool becomes harmful once its numbers are treated as proof rather than estimates.

Leon takes over “Priority Number One Again,” issuing incompatible demands with no
responsibility for completing them. “Who Moved My Ticket?” plays like a short office
mystery: an inconvenient issue vanishes because leaving it visible would damage the
dashboard. The joke leads directly into “Velocity Is Mandatory.” Victor sees a team
complete forty points and orders eighty for the next sprint. A rigid, martial pulse makes
the arithmetic sound less like ambition than discipline.

The album’s central set piece is “Sprint Planning Eternity.” The meeting lasts fourteen
hours, and the arrangement keeps losing its way through meter changes, time stamps, and
overlapping voices. Sandwiches arrive. Daylight leaves the room. Cleaners pass the windows
while the team continues estimating work that has not been defined. The track is funny at
first, then deliberately tiring. It lets the ritual consume the same time the characters
are supposed to protect.

“Blocked by Management” removes the ambiguity. Everyone knows where the delay sits, but no
one is allowed to name it. “The Retrospective of Blame” then converts a meeting meant for
reflection into a search for individual fault. By this point, terms such as trust,
transparency, and accountability have become shields for the people least willing to be
examined.

Sabine confronts her own part in the system on “Agile in Name Only.” The quieter
arrangement gives her recognition some weight: she has used collaborative language to
enforce decisions the team did not make. Her response begins with “No More Meetings About
Meetings,” where developers, QA, and operations cancel rituals that consume time without
improving the product. The resistance is collective, not a sudden act of individual
heroism.

The title track delivers the expected power-metal scale while keeping the revenge
practical. Sabine removes rankings, compulsory calendar events, output comparisons, and
dashboard fields used to set employees against one another. The plastic “Impediment
Remover” sword survives as a joke, but the decisive act is administrative: the team stops
feeding a system that confuses control with progress.

“Build Something That Works” closes on a smaller victory. No presentation saves the
company. The team repairs a real fault, replaces failing hardware, tests the change, and
releases it. The five-beat timer returns as a calmer melody. Structure was never the
enemy; the problem was a structure designed to monitor work instead of helping people do
it.
