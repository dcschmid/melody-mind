## About the Album

Tuhansien Järvien Laulut begins with departure, but it is not really an escape record.
"Kaupunki Jäi Taakse" leaves the city behind because the album needs space: enough silence
for old voices, water, tape hiss, and the kind of family memory that does not arrive in a
straight line.

"Vanha Kesätalo" brings the listener to the house at the center of the record. The song is
not interested in postcard Finland. It notices ordinary things: wood that has held many
seasons, a room that smells closed at first, the slight shock of returning to a place that
remembers you as someone younger. "Isoäidin Kasetit" then gives the album its most
intimate device. The cassettes do not behave like a perfect archive. They crackle, blur,
repeat, and make memory feel physical.

The early middle of the album moves through inherited history. "Sodan Järvi" is careful
with war; it treats it as a pressure left in the family rather than a dramatic costume.
"Ensimmäinen Juhannus" opens the windows a little, letting midsummer light into a record
that otherwise prefers dusk. "Hiljainen Avioliitto" is quieter and more severe, built
around the idea that love can last for years without becoming easy to speak about.

"Talven Kirjeet" returns to paper, distance, and weather. The song works because it lets
winter slow everything down. "Mustikan Tuoksu" is more immediate: the smell of berries
does more for the album than another grand statement about childhood would. "Saunan
Jälkeen" has the same plain strength. It places feeling after heat, water, and breathing,
where people sometimes tell the truth because there is no formal moment for it.

The second half darkens without forcing melodrama. "Katoavat Saaret" is about loss that
happens gradually, until the map no longer matches what the family remembers. "Pimeän
Veden Alla" moves below the surface, into grief that is sensed more than explained. The
title track, "Tuhannen Järven Laulu," widens the record again, but it does not turn the
lakes into a slogan. Each lake feels like a different kind of silence.

"Kun Järvi Muistaa" is the album's strongest late answer: memory is not only in people,
but in repeated places, in water crossed each summer, in paths to a dock, in rituals that
outlive the people who taught them. "Paluu Valoon" closes with light, but the light feels
earned because the album has spent so much time in half-shadow. It is not a cure for
grief. It is a way to live after listening.

Tuhansien Järvien Laulut is at its best when it stays small. Acoustic guitars, soft piano,
restrained drums, warm electric tones, and lake-like ambience leave room around the songs.
The record understands that family history often survives in objects before it becomes
language: tapes, letters, smoke, berries, water, and the door of an old summer house
opening again.
