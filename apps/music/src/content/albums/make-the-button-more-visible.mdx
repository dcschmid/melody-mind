## About the Album

At Infinite Loop Solutions, Project Phoenix has fourteen approved shades of blue and no
obvious way to complete a basic task. UX designer Amelia Park is brought in to fix it. Her
first move is simple: sit with customers, watch where they hesitate, and record what sends
them back to the previous screen. The findings point to a product organized around the
company rather than the people using it. Department names have become menu labels, icons
change meaning between pages, and years of special requests compete for attention.

That setup gives Make the Button More Visible a strong comic engine, but the joke has a
target. This is an album about who gets heard when a product is failing. Amelia arrives
with observed behavior; the board answers with color preferences, branding demands, and
another feature for the home screen. No one is openly against clarity. They simply keep
adding conditions until clarity is impossible.

The music follows the argument. Art-rock structures leave room for abrupt turns, repeated
phrases, and sections that interrupt one another like people in a crowded review meeting.
Alternative-metal guitars add weight when disagreement hardens into authority, while
shorter new-wave patterns bring a colder, more procedural feel to the office scenes. The
record stays tense without treating every track as a crisis.

“Fourteen Shades of Blue” opens inside the clutter. Angular guitar figures and nervous
rhythmic changes make each new option feel less useful than the last. “Nobody Finds the
Menu” moves outside the design team and watches capable customers blame themselves for
software they cannot navigate. That shift matters. The problem is no longer an untidy
screen; it is the loss of confidence produced by a system that refuses to explain itself.

The title track supplies the obvious hook and then wears it down through repetition. “Make
the button more visible” sounds reasonable in isolation. Within the song, it becomes a way
to avoid harder questions: What will the button do? Which account will it affect? Why is
the action buried in the first place? Clipped responses and rising guitars turn a routine
piece of feedback into a fight over the definition of the problem.

The next run of songs gives each source of interference its own shape. “The Logo Needs
More Space” carries Victor’s certainty on a heavy bass line. “Every Customer Is Unique”
follows Sales as customer care becomes an argument for endless customization. “Design by
Committee” breaks into competing rhythms and overlapping instructions. The arrangement
does not merely describe a crowded prototype; it behaves like one.

“Can We Add One More Tab?” pushes the running joke close to exhaustion before the record
changes perspective. On “The User Test” and “Watch Them Fail,” the music opens up and the
board sits behind glass while customers attempt familiar tasks. One participant apologizes
for not being good with systems. The line lands harder than the jokes because the listener
already knows who is responsible for the confusion.

Amelia answers with “Research Is Not an Opinion,” the album’s most direct
alternative-metal track. Its point is narrower than the title first suggests. Taste still
has a place in design, but a preferred color or logo size cannot erase repeated failure in
testing. The song works because Amelia is not claiming personal authority. She is asking
the company to look at what happened on the screen.

The final act is about repair. “Build the System” introduces Orbit, a shared set of
components and interaction rules. Earlier fragments begin to line up, and the recurring
four-note motif finally reaches a stable form. “Remove What Nobody Needs” is equally
important: duplicated reports, obsolete shortcuts, and unsupported custom features leave
the product because they no longer serve a task. The simplification feels earned rather
than decorative.

Customers move through “The First Clear Screen” without private notes or memorized
workarounds. There is no miraculous invention behind the improvement. The team listened,
tested, removed, and standardized. Then “Could the Button Be Larger?” brings back the
request that started the argument. This time Amelia does not need to defeat it in a
meeting. The team can test the change and keep it only if it helps.
