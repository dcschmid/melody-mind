## About the Album

No Empires Left to Burn is a protest record that distrusts tidy political language. Its
songs are full of maps, ports, borders, flags, broadcast rooms, and small countries spoken
about by people who do not have to live with the consequences. The guitars are dirty, the
drums are dry, and the gang vocals sound less like unity than pressure finally finding a
throat.

"Maps Are Not Yours" opens with the album's simplest argument. A map is not an empty
surface waiting for a signature; it is already full of homes, graves, roads, names, and
languages. "Empire in a Suit" follows by changing the costume. The old hunger for control
appears in clean rooms, polite phrases, and economic language that tries to make
domination sound administrative.

"Greenland Burns Cold" uses contradiction well: a frozen image carrying heat, extraction,
strategy, and threat. "Panama Ghostline" works like a scar on a trade route, a reminder
that channels and corridors are never only technical. "The 51st Lie" brings the record
closer to propaganda, where numbers and slogans are used to make absorption sound
inevitable.

The middle section is where the album sharpens. "Borders Have Blood" refuses the clean
line. "Trade War Hymn" makes commerce sound like a marching song, which is exactly the
point: markets can be made to carry the rhythm of conflict. "Flag Merchants" is one of the
stronger titles here because it turns nationalism into a business model. The flag is not
just raised; it is packaged, sold, and waved on cue.

"No King Across the Sea" gives the record a direct anti-imperial shout without
overcomplicating it. "Small Nations, Big Shadows" is quieter and better for it. The song
understands that pressure is not always invasion. Sometimes it is a meeting, a debt, a
military phrase, a port agreement, a headline, or a leader speaking as if a smaller
country were an object in the room.

"You Can’t Annex a Soul" is the emotional center because it moves away from territory and
toward personhood. The album's anger lands hardest when it names what power cannot own:
memory, language, grief, family, accent, prayer, jokes, street names, and the habit of
calling a place home. "Broadcasts from the Burning Room" turns that resistance into
transmission. The room is burning, but the signal is still leaving it.

The title track does not sound like victory. "No Empires Left to Burn" is more like a
refusal to keep feeding the old machine. The closing song, "The People Live Here," is the
right ending because it brings the record back from strategy to address. Not resources.
Not leverage. Not a bargaining chip. People live here.

No Empires Left to Burn is blunt, but not careless. Its best songs know that anti-empire
music needs more than raised fists. It needs places, documents, borders, broadcasts,
markets, and the ordinary human detail that proves why the map was never empty.
