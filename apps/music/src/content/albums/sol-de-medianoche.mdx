## About This Album

Sol de Medianoche is a 14-track record in Spanish. The palette is familiar from classic
Afro-Cuban and Latin jazz — congas, brushed drums, warm upright bass, piano in close
voicings, muted brass, nylon guitar — but the execution leans modern: cleaner production,
softer dynamics, longer shadows. No track punches through. Everything sits back, breathes,
and waits.

The album has no interest in spectacle. The rhythms syncopate gently rather than driving
hard. The brass is muted more often than open. The piano keeps to small rooms. Ambient
textures appear in some tracks, sparse and controlled. The Spanish vocals move within the
arrangements rather than over them — closer to a whisper than a performance.

Each track has its own weight. "Luna en la Habana" is cooler, more open. "Jardín de Humo"
drifts and darkens. "Café a las Doce" is the warmest moment — the kind of track that works
at 3am with the windows open. The sequencing pulls the listener through different rooms
and temperatures, not as a narrative but as a mood that shifts and returns.

The album works best as a full listen. Individual tracks carry, but the record earns its
length by not losing the thread. By track seven or eight the atmosphere is established
enough that the remaining tracks feel inevitable rather than padded. "Rosa del Aire"
closes it quietly — the shortest track, the one that should have come first.

This is not a genre record. It does not reach back to a specific decade or lean on vintage
gear to announce its influences. The jazz and Latin elements are real but softened. The
result sits closer to a late-night soundtrack than to any live tradition.

All tracks were generated with AI music models. The files were processed and mastered for
a warm, controlled low-end. No human performance recordings were used.
