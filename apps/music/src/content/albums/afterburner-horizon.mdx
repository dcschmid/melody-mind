## About the Album

Afterburner Horizon is structured like a flight log rather than a collection of isolated
action cues. Its fourteen tracks move from pre-dawn preparation to launch, interception,
combat, and return. That sequence gives the record its tension: the mission begins before
the aircraft leaves the runway, and it continues after the obvious danger has passed.

The title track establishes the scale with brass, strings, and heavy percussion. "Scramble
Before Sunrise" and "Runway Burn" then compress the pace around readiness and
acceleration. Their job is direct, but the album does not remain on the runway for long.
"Carrier Wake" moves the setting offshore, while "Black Water Intercept" and "Heat
Signature" narrow the story to detection and pursuit.

The middle of the record is its most sustained action passage. "Low Altitude Vector" keeps
the imagined flight close to the ground; "Storm Front Corridor" adds weather as another
source of pressure. "Fox Two," "Knife-Edge Dogfight," and "No-Fail Airspace" use clipped
titles and compact running times to maintain the sense of a mission in which decisions
arrive faster than reflection.

Those titles form a technical vocabulary without attaching the score to a named pilot or
specific conflict. The album stays with procedures and conditions: a heat signature on a
screen, a low-altitude route, restricted airspace, black water below. That choice keeps
the story open while giving every cue a practical role. The listener follows the operation
through its stages rather than receiving a conventional hero narrative.

The fourteen cues also hold to a narrow range of roughly three to four minutes. None has
to carry an entire set piece alone. Instead, tension accumulates through handoffs: runway
to carrier, carrier to intercept, storm to dogfight, dogfight to the return path. This
compact construction suits the subject. Aviation depends on sequences of precise actions,
and the album adopts the same discipline in its track order.

That reflection finally enters with "Above the Thunderline." The cue opens space after the
denser middle section and changes altitude from a tactical advantage into a moment of
distance. "Smoke on the Return Path" carries the aftermath into the homeward leg. The
mission may be over, but the return does not sound uncomplicated.

The repeated horizon image frames that change. In the title track, the horizon is a line
to cross at speed. By "Smoke on the Return Path," it has become the route home and a place
to measure what the mission has cost. The final cue turns it to fire. Reusing one image in
three different contexts gives the otherwise anonymous operation a clear dramatic shape.

"Horizon of Fire" closes the album by bringing its large orchestral language back into
view. The finale works because the preceding tracks have established more than speed:
briefing, exposure, uncertainty, and consequence are all part of the flight. Afterburner
Horizon remains an action score, but its pacing gives the machinery a human frame. The
return occupies two cues, treating survival as a stage of the mission rather than its
automatic conclusion or clean resolution.
