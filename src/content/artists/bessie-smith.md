name: Bessie Smith

biography: Bessie Smith was the most successful and highest-paid African American entertainer of the 1920s and 1930s, earning her the title "Empress of the Blues." Born in Chattanooga, Tennessee, she began her career performing on the vaudeville circuit and quickly rose to fame with her powerful contralto voice and emotionally charged performances. Her recording career spanned from 1923 to 1933, during which she made over 160 recordings. Smith played a crucial role in the development of blues music and influenced generations of singers across all genres. Her tragic death after a car accident in Clarksdale, Mississippi, marked the end of the classic blues era.

Her recordings have been continuously reissued and remain bestsellers in the blues genre.
careerTimeline:

Her early childhood in Chattanooga, located along the Tennessee River in the heart of the American South, exposed her to the rich musical traditions of African American culture including spirituals, work songs, and the blues that were developing in the rural South during this period. The challenges of growing up Black in the segregated South would later inform her powerful artistic voice."

This early exposure to professional entertainment provided invaluable training in audience engagement and performance dynamics that would serve her throughout her career. The vaudeville circuit, while limiting in artistic scope, gave her practical experience."
  - year: "1913"

Gee's involvement in her career helped organize her performances and business affairs during her early rise to fame, though their marriage would be marked by infidelity and eventual separation. Despite the personal challenges, this period saw her development."
  - year: "1920"
    event: "Established herself as a rising blues star on the theater circuit, performing in African American theaters throughout the South and building the local reputation that would soon attract national attention. Her powerful contralto voice and dynamic stage presence distinguished her from other performers and earned her a loyal following among Black audiences who flocked to see her shows. This period of regional success provided the foundation."
  - year: "1923"
    event: "Recorded 'Downhearted Blues,' her first hit with Columbia Records, which sold an unprecedented 780,000 copies and established her as a recording star of unprecedented magnitude for a Black female artist during the height of the Jazz Age. The song's success was remarkable given the limited marketing resources available to promote recordings by African American artists and demonstrated the existence of a massive untapped market for blues music. Its success transformed her career."
  - year: "1924"
    event: "Signed with Columbia Records and became their highest-selling artist, earning $2,500 per side recorded and becoming the highest-paid Black artist in the country during an era of widespread racial discrimination and limited opportunities for African American entertainers. Her commercial success for Columbia demonstrated that investment in Black artists could yield substantial returns and challenged the industry's assumptions about market limitations. This deal transformed her financial status."
  - year: "1925"
    event: "Recorded 'Alexander's Ragtime Band' with Louis Armstrong, creating one of the most significant collaborations in jazz history that brought together two of the era's most important African American musical innovators. The session, which featured Armstrong's distinctive cornet playing alongside Smith's powerful vocals, demonstrated the potential for meaningful artistic collaboration across different performance styles. The recording became an instant classic."
  - year: "1926"
    event: "Released 'Stormy Weather,' which became one of her signature songs and demonstrated her ability to convey deep emotional complexity through her nuanced interpretations of song lyrics and vocal performance. The song, later recorded by countless artists, became associated with her powerful delivery and emotional authenticity. Its enduring popularity confirmed her interpretive skills."
  - year: "1927"
    event: "Reached peak commercial success with multiple hit recordings including 'After You've Gone,' 'Sentimental Blues,' and 'Yodeling Blues,' establishing herself as the undisputed queen of blues music and the most successful female recording artist of the decade. Her recordings during this period sold millions of copies and made her the highest-paid African American entertainer of her era, a remarkable achievement given the racial and gender barriers she faced. This commercial success validated her artistic choices."

She remained one of the few artists."

The recordings demonstrated her continuing power."

She continued to perform to devoted audiences."

Her funeral in New York City drew thousands."
