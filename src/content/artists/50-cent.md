biography: "Curtis James Jackson III, known professionally as 50 Cent, born on July 6, 1975, in Queens, New York, is an American rapper, songwriter, and entrepreneur who rose to prominence in the early 2000s as one of hip-hop's most commercially successful artists. He achieved mainstream breakthrough in 2003 with the mixtape Guess Who's Back? and the album Get Rich or Die Tryin' (2003), which debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and became one of the best-selling debut albums of all time with over 12 million copies sold in the United States. His blend of gangsta rap, pop sensibility, and entrepreneurial ventures including his G-Unit record label and business investments made him one of the highest-paid celebrities in the world. Jackson has sold over 30 million records worldwide and has successfully transitioned into acting and entrepreneurship."

careerTimeline:
  - year: "1975"
    event: "Born Curtis James Jackson III in Queens, New York on July 6, raised in the South Jamaica neighborhood by his mother Sabrina Jackson. South Jamaica was one of New York City's most notorious housing projects, known for its high crime rates and economic challenges that would deeply shape his worldview. His early life in this environment provided the raw material for the gritty, street-level storytelling that would later define his music and persona."
  - year: "1988"
    event: "At age 13, began dealing crack cocaine after his mother died of cancer, entering the dangerous world of street crime to support himself financially. His mother's death left him orphaned and homeless at a young age, forcing him to become self-reliant in one of America's toughest urban environments. This period of his life would later provide the authentic voice and credibility that distinguished him from other rappers who glamorized street life without having lived it."
  - year: "1992"
    event: "Started rapping at age 16, recording his first demo tracks and developing his unique storytelling style influenced by Nas, Rakim, and the golden age of East Coast hip-hop. He began performing at local jams and open mic nights in Queens, gradually building a reputation for his vivid lyrical content and commanding delivery. During this formative period, he developed the conversational flow and conversational storytelling technique that would become his signature style."
  - year: "1994"
    event: "Signed his first major record deal with Columbia Records, preparing to launch his music career with the backing of a major label. This deal came after years of developing his skills and building local buzz through performances and underground recordings. However, the label executives were initially hesitant to fully commit to his vision, leading to a protracted development period that would delay his debut."
  - year: "1996"
    event: "Was discovered by Jam Master Jay of Run DMC, the legendary hip-hop pioneer who helped him develop his sound and signed him to his JMJ Records label. Jam Master Jay recognized his raw talent and potential, taking him under his wing and providing professional guidance that transformed his raw abilities into commercial-ready material. This mentorship was particularly significant given Jam Master Jay's status as one of hip-hop's founding fathers and his credibility in the industry."
  - year: "1999"
    event: "Was shot nine times in a drive-by shooting in his neighborhood, miraculously surviving and spending weeks in hospital recovering from life-threatening injuries. The attack targeted him specifically, with bullets striking his face, hands, and legs in what police believed was a retaliation for his growing reputation in the drug trade. His survival became legendary in hip-hop circles and would later be incorporated into his public persona as evidence of his resilience and divine purpose."
  - year: "2000"
    event: "Released his debut album Power of the Dollar under Columbia Records, which was shelved after his shooting incident and never received a commercial release. The album showcased his evolution as an artist and contained tracks that would later become hits when re-released on his follow-up albums. Its shelving was a devastating blow to his career momentum and left him without a record deal or clear path forward."
  - year: "2002"
    event: "Released the underground hit 'Wanksta' on his mixtape, gaining national attention and attracting major label interest from executives eager to sign the next hip-hop superstar. The song's success demonstrated his ability to craft commercially appealing tracks while maintaining his street credibility and raw authenticity. This underground hit served as his audition tape for the major labels that would eventually compete for his signature."
  - year: "2002"
    event: "Signed with Eminem's Shady Records and Dr. Dre's Aftermath Entertainment in a landmark deal that launched his major mainstream career with unprecedented industry support. Eminem discovered him through the 'Wanksta' single and immediately recognized his commercial potential, personally championing his signing to the joint venture. This partnership with two of hip-hop's most successful artists and producers provided him with the resources, distribution, and artistic guidance needed to finally release his debut."
  - year: "2003"
    event: "Released 'Guess Who's Back?' mixtape followed by debut album Get Rich or Die Tryin', which debuted at #1 on the Billboard 200 and sold over 872,000 copies in its first week, the strongest debut for a new artist in SoundScan history. The album's success exceeded all industry expectations and signaled the arrival of hip-hop's new commercial king. Its combination of gangsta rap credibility, pop accessibility, and Eminem's production created a formula that dominated the music landscape."
  - year: "2003"
    event: "'In Da Club' became a global phenomenon, topping charts in over 15 countries and establishing 50 Cent as a cultural icon whose influence extended far beyond music into fashion, language, and youth culture. The song's innovative music video, featuring him dancing in a wheelchair with Eminem and Dr. Dre, became iconic and was heavily rotated on MTV and BET. Its unprecedented crossover success demonstrated hip-hop's growing dominance of the global popular music landscape."
  - year: "2004"
    event: "Founded G-Unit Records, building his own empire and signing artists like Lloyd Banks, Young Buck, and Tony Yayo who would form his crew and dominate hip-hop radio. This entrepreneurial move demonstrated his understanding of the music industry's economics and his desire to control his own destiny as an artist and businessman would become one of. G-Unit hip-hop's most successful and influential crews of the 2000s."
  - year: "2005"
    event: "Released The Massacre, which sold over 1.15 million copies in its first week, solidifying his commercial dominance and making him one of the few artists to achieve million-plus first-week sales multiple times. The album's success was particularly remarkable given the changing music landscape of the mid-2000s with illegal downloading threatening album sales industry-wide. His continued commercial viability during this challenging period established him as hip-hop's most reliable hitmaker."
  - year: "2005"
    event: "'Candy Shop' became his fourth #1 single on the Billboard Hot 100, demonstrating his crossover appeal and pop sensibilities that made him unique among his rap contemporaries. The song, featuring Olivia, showcased his ability to craft party anthems that appealed to both hip-hop purists and mainstream pop audiences. This string of chart-topping hits cemented his status as a crossover superstar capable of dominating every format."
  - year: "2007"
    event: "Released Curtis album in a highly publicized release date battle with Kanye West's Graduation, one of the most talked-about hip-hop showdowns in history that generated massive media attention and sales for both artists. Despite losing the battle in terms of first-week sales, the album still debuted at #2 and went platinum, demonstrating his commercial resilience. This competition highlighted his status as one of hip-hop's premier artists capable of headlining major release events."
  - year: "2008"
    event: "Made his acting debut in the film Get Rich or Die Tryin', a semi-autobiographical movie about his life story directed by Jim Sheridan and co-starring Jennifer Morrison and Terrence Howard. The film attempted to translate his life story into a conventional Hollywood narrative, exploring his rise from the streets of Queens to international superstardom. While reviews were mixed, the film was a commercial success and demonstrated his potential as a film star."
  - year: "2009"
    event: "Expanded his business empire with strategic investments in Vitaminwater (earning $100 million when Coca-Cola acquired the company), headphones, and other ventures that made him one of the wealthiest celebrities in hip-hop. His business acumen was recognized by Forbes magazine, which featured him on its Celebrity 100 list and highlighted his unusual ability to generate income from sources beyond music. These ventures transformed him from a rapper into a full-fledged entrepreneur and brand."
  - year: "2010"
    event: "Released Animal Ambition, his first independent album released through his own label, demonstrating his ability to operate successfully outside the major label system. The album's release was accompanied by an innovative marketing campaign that included a documentary about his creative process. This independent release showed his evolution as an artist comfortable with controlling his own releases."
  - year: "2014"
    event: "Released Street King Immortal after years of delays and label disputes, finally delivering the major label follow-up to his earlier success stories despite continued challenges in the modern music landscape. The album, featuring collaborations with Eminem, Dr. Dre, and others, attempted to recapture the magic of his earlier work while adapting to changed industry conditions. Its release demonstrated his continued relevance in an industry increasingly dominated by streaming and younger artists."
  - year: "2019"
    event: "Invested in and promoted cryptocurrency, demonstrating his continued interest in innovative business ventures and his willingness to embrace emerging technologies and financial opportunities. His high-profile promotion of Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies attracted both praise for his entrepreneurial spirit and criticism from financial regulators. This latest venture showed his constant drive to stay ahead of business trends."
  - year: "2020s"
    event: "Continued to influence hip-hop culture through his business ventures, social media presence where he maintains millions of followers, and mentorship of younger artists looking to follow in his entrepreneurial footsteps. He also expanded into television and film production, developing content through his G-Unit Films banner. His influence on hip-hop's evolution from purely musical enterprise to multimedia brand empire remains his most lasting legacy."
