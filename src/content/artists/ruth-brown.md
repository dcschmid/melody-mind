biography: "Ruth Brown, born Ruth Alston Weston, was an American singer-songwriter and actress known as 'Miss R&B' and 'Queen of R&B.' A pioneering R&B vocalist, Brown recorded for Atlantic Records from 1949 to 1962, releasing over 20 R&B chart hits including 8 number-one singles: 'Teardrops from My Eyes,' 'So Long,' 'I'll Wait for You,' and '(Mama) He Treats Your Daughter Mean.' Her powerful voice and dynamic stage presence influenced countless singers including Etta James, Aretha Franklin, and LaVern Baker. A Grammy Award winner and Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inductee, Brown also pioneered R&B radio and successfully transitioned to Broadway and film acting, appearing in 'Hairspray' and 'The Blues Brothers.' Her songs have sold over 10 million copies worldwide."

careerTimeline:
  - year: "1945"
    event: "Began performing with Lucky Millinder Orchestra, one of the most prominent big bands of the era featuring future stars like Quincy Jones and Charles Mingus; made her first radio appearances on major networks. Working with Millinder's orchestra provided invaluable training and industry connections that proved essential to her later success."
  - year: "1947"
    event: "Won a prestigious talent contest at Club Harlem in Washington, D.C., a legendary venue that had launched numerous African American entertainers; signed with Atlantic Records immediately after the competition. The signing was brokered by record executive Ahmet Ertegun and his partner Herb Abramson, who recognized her exceptional vocal talent and commercial potential. This contract marked the beginning of a recording partnership that would produce some of the most influential R&B recordings of the era."
  - year: "1949"
    event: "Released debut single 'So Long' on Atlantic Records, which became her first major hit and established her as a rising star in the emerging R&B market. The song demonstrated her powerful voice and emotional delivery that distinguished her from contemporaries. This release initiated a string of successes that would establish Atlantic Records as a major force in R&B music."
  - year: "1950"
    event: "Released 'Teardrops from My Eyes,' which topped the R&B chart and became her first number-one hit, remaining at the summit for an impressive 11 weeks; established her as Atlantic Records' flagship artist. The song's success demonstrated the growing commercial power of African American music audiences during the post-war period. This breakthrough established the template for her subsequent string of hits and confirmed her status as a major recording artist."
  - year: "1951"
    event: "Released 'I'll Wait for You' and 'Lucky Lips,' continuing her remarkable chart dominance with multiple hits demonstrating her remarkable consistency and audience appeal. These recordings showcased her developing interpretive skills and ability to convey deep emotion through her powerful voice. The continued success solidified her position as one of the most reliable hitmakers in the R&B field."
  - year: "1952"
    event: "Released '5-10-15 Hours' and 'Baby, You've Got What It Takes,' the latter becoming a number-one hit that exemplified her dynamic vocal performances and the exciting new sound of R&B. 'Baby, You've Got What It Takes' was recorded with her longtime accompanist, the legendary guitarist Freddie Brown (no relation), whose sophisticated playing complemented her powerful vocals. These recordings established her as a cornerstone artist for Atlantic Records' dominance in the R&B market."
  - year: "1953"
    event: "Released '(Mama) He Treats Your Daughter Mean,' which became one of her signature songs and demonstrated her ability to convey complex emotions with raw honesty and powerful delivery. The song's narrative of female defiance and independence resonated with Black audiences who rarely saw their experiences portrayed in popular music. This track became a feminist anthem that influenced generations of female artists who followed."
  - year: "1954"
    event: "Released 'I Can Hear the Blues Calling,' demonstrating her versatility and ability to interpret traditional blues material with authentic emotional depth while maintaining her modern R&B sensibility. The recording showcased her sophisticated understanding of blues phrasing and emotional nuance. This artistic growth revealed an artist committed to musical excellence rather than mere chart success."
  - year: "1955"
    event: "Released 'Love Didn't Lie' and 'I Don't Hurt Anymore,' the latter showing her increasing maturity as an interpreter capable of conveying subtle emotional complexity. These recordings demonstrated her evolution from pure R&B toward a more nuanced artistic expression. The releases maintained her commercial success while showcasing her artistic development as a serious vocalist."

    event: "Released 'Wild Wild Young Men' and continued her dominance of the R&B charts, with both songs becoming major hits that captured the excitement and energy of the rock and roll era. 'Wild Wild Young Men' celebrated the emerging youth culture and freedom of the mid-1950s. These releases proved that established R&B artists could successfully adapt to the changing musical landscape."

The album demonstrated her ability to sustain interest across an entire record rather than relying solely on hit singles. This release established her as a serious recording artist capable of artistic statement."
  - year: "1959"

The album showcased her mature artistic vision and confident vocal performances. This release confirmed her status as one of the most important recording artists of her generation."

The departure marked the end of an era for both Brown and the label that had been built substantially around her success. This transition reflected the changing landscape of the recording industry as the rock and roll era matured."
